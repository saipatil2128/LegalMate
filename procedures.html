What to Bring With You:

  • Your written complaint with all important facts (plaint)

Helpful Tip: You must file your case either where the person you're suing lives OR where the dispute happened. Filing in the wrong place could get your case dismissed!

How Long Things Take:

  • Other party's response: Usually within 30 days (can extend to 90 days)
  • Sharing documents with each other: As scheduled by the court
  • Witness statements and questioning: Takes several hearings
  • Final arguments: After all evidence is presented
Important Note: Civil cases in India often take years to resolve completely. Business-related disputes now have a special "fast track" process under the Commercial Courts Act.
What if the other party doesn't respond to the summons?
If the other party doesn't file a written response after receiving proper notice, the court can proceed with an "ex-parte" decision, which means the case continues without their input, and you may win by default.
Do I need a lawyer for a civil case?
While you can represent yourself (called "party-in-person"), having a lawyer is highly recommended because civil procedures are complex. For smaller claims, you can approach consumer forums or lok adalats where procedures are simpler.

How to Appeal if You Lose Your Case

Where to Appeal:

  • If you lost in District Court → Appeal to High Court
  • If you lost in High Court → Appeal to Supreme Court

Time Limits for Filing Appeals:

  • 30 days from judgment date for most appeals
  • 90 days for Supreme Court appeals
  • Extra time possible with good reason for delay

What You Need for Appeal:

  • Appeal application (called "Memorandum of Appeal")
  • Official copy of the judgment you're challenging
  • Clear reasons why the judgment was wrong
  • Required court fees
Helpful Tip: An appeal is NOT a complete rehearing of your case. You need to show specific legal errors in the original judgment. New evidence is generally not allowed unless there's a very good reason why it wasn't available earlier.
Alternative Option: Before filing a formal appeal, consider a "Review Petition" in the same court that gave the judgment. This is quicker and less expensive, but only works if there's an obvious error or new important evidence.

What You Need to Know:

  • FIR must be registered for serious crimes (like theft, assault, fraud)
  • "Zero FIR" can be filed at any police station if the crime happened elsewhere
  • Police must complete investigation within 90 days for serious crimes
  • If police take too long, accused person can get automatic bail
If Police Refuse to File Your FIR: You can approach the Superintendent of Police or file a complaint directly with a Magistrate under Section 156(3) or Section 200 of the Criminal Procedure Code. Always get a receipt when filing a police complaint.

Different Types of Criminal Trials:

  • Warrant Trial: For serious crimes punishable by more than 2 years in prison
  • Summons Trial: For less serious offenses with simpler procedures
  • Summary Trial: For very minor offenses with quick procedures
Special Courts: Certain cases like child abuse (POCSO), corruption, terrorism, etc., have their own special courts with modified procedures.
What are my rights if I'm accused of a crime?
You have the right to: remain silent, know the charges against you, consult a lawyer (free legal aid if you can't afford one), not be forced to confess, cross-examine witnesses, present your own evidence, and be presumed innocent until proven guilty beyond reasonable doubt.
Can I withdraw a criminal complaint?
For certain "compoundable" offenses (like simple hurt, defamation, or some property offenses), you can settle with the accused and withdraw the case with court permission. However, serious crimes (murder, rape, etc.) cannot be withdrawn even if the victim wants to, as these are considered crimes against society.

Understanding Bail - Getting Released During Trial

Types of Bail:

  • Regular Bail: When someone is already arrested (Sections 437/439)
  • Anticipatory Bail: When someone fears arrest (Section 438)
  • Default Bail: When police don't complete investigation on time (Section 167(2))
  • Interim Bail: Temporary bail until final bail decision

How to Apply for Bail:

  • For minor offenses: Apply to the police officer or Magistrate's court
  • For serious offenses: Apply to Magistrate or Sessions Court
  • For anticipatory bail: Apply to Sessions Court or High Court

Common Bail Conditions:

  • Sign a bond promising to attend all court dates
  • Provide "sureties" (people who guarantee you'll attend court)
  • Surrender your passport in some cases
  • Report to police station regularly
  • Not contact witnesses or tamper with evidence
Helpful Tip: Bail is a RIGHT for most offenses, not a privilege. For non-violent, bailable offenses, courts are more likely to grant bail. Factors that help: having a fixed address, family ties, no criminal record, and willingness to follow conditions.
Important: Breaking bail conditions can result in immediate arrest and difficulty getting bail again in the future.

Writ Petitions - Challenging Government Actions

Types of Writs Made Simple:

  • Habeas Corpus: When someone is wrongfully detained or arrested
  • Mandamus: To make government officials do their legal duty
  • Prohibition: To stop a lower court from overstepping its powers
  • Certiorari: To cancel an improper order from a lower court or tribunal
  • Quo Warranto: To challenge someone wrongfully holding a public office

How to File a Writ:

  • Prepare petition clearly stating which right was violated
  • File in High Court (under Article 226) or Supreme Court (under Article 32)
  • Include all relevant documents and specific grounds
  • Pay required court fees
When to Use Writs: Writs are powerful tools when government officials or courts have clearly violated your legal rights or acted beyond their powers. They're faster than regular cases but should be filed quickly after the violation (ideally within a few months).

Public Interest Litigation (PIL) - Fighting for Broader Issues

What You Need to File a PIL:

  • The issue must affect the general public or disadvantaged groups
  • Any concerned citizen can file, not just directly affected people
  • Even a simple letter to the Chief Justice can be treated as a PIL
  • Less strict rules compared to regular cases

Steps to File a PIL:

  • Write a detailed petition explaining the public interest issue
  • Support with basic facts and supporting documents
  • File with a sworn statement (affidavit) confirming the facts
  • The court may appoint a helper (amicus curiae) to assist
  • Court often gives direct orders to authorities to solve the problem
What Makes a Good PIL: PILs work best for major issues that affect many people but aren't being addressed, like environmental problems, human rights violations, or government corruption. The courts take PILs seriously when they involve fundamental rights or serious government negligence.
Be Careful: Courts have become strict about PILs to prevent misuse. Your PIL may be rejected or fined if it's for personal gain, political motives, or issues that can be handled by regular legal processes.
Can I file a PIL without a lawyer?
Yes, you can file a PIL without a lawyer. In fact, the Supreme Court even accepts PILs through letters addressed to the Chief Justice. However, having legal help is recommended to properly frame the issue and follow procedures, especially for complex matters.

Specialized Tribunals - Expert Forums for Specific Issues

Major Tribunals in India:

  • National Green Tribunal: For environmental cases
  • Central Administrative Tribunal: For government employee matters
  • National Company Law Tribunal: For corporate disputes
  • Income Tax Appellate Tribunal: For tax disagreements
  • Consumer Forums: For complaints against products and services

How Tribunals Work:

  • Fill out specific application forms (each tribunal has its own)
  • Simpler and less formal than regular courts
  • Cases are decided more quickly, with specific timeframes
  • If you're unhappy with tribunal decision, you can appeal to High Court or Supreme Court
Advantages of Tribunals: Tribunals have expert members who understand specialized fields (like environment, tax, or banking). They're usually faster and less expensive than regular courts. Many allow you to represent yourself without a lawyer.
Consumer Forums - Easy Access to Justice: Consumer forums are one of the most accessible tribunals for ordinary citizens. You can file complaints about defective products, poor services, unfair trade practices, or overcharging. There are three levels: District (up to ₹1 crore), State (up to ₹10 crores), and National (above ₹10 crores).
How do I choose between regular court and a tribunal?
If your issue falls within a tribunal's special area (like environmental matters for NGT or service matters for CAT), the tribunal is usually the better choice - they're faster, have subject experts, and follow simpler procedures. In some cases, the law requires you to approach the tribunal first before going to regular courts.

Practical Court Tips For Everyone

How to Prepare Court Documents

Simple guidelines to make sure your court papers are accepted.

  • Use A4 size white paper with 1.5 line spacing
  • Number all pages properly and create an index
  • Pay correct court fees (ask court clerk if unsure)
  • Make enough copies for court and all parties

Court Etiquette & Behavior

How to act in court to make a good impression.

  • Dress formally (white shirt/saree preferred)
  • Stand when the judge enters or when speaking
  • Address the judge as "Your Honor" or "Honorable Court"
  • Speak clearly and don't interrupt others
  • Switch off your mobile phone

Court Fees & Costs

Understanding what you'll need to pay.

  • Claims up to ₹1,000: Court fee is approximately ₹20–₹50
  • Claims between ₹1,001 and ₹10,000: Fees range from ₹50–₹500
  • Claims between ₹10,001 and ₹1,00,000: Fees range from ₹500–₹5,000
  • Claims above ₹1,00,000: Approximately 1% of the claim amount, subject to a state-specific maximum (e.g., ₹75,000 in some states)
  • Additional fees may apply for applications, certified copies, and miscellaneous petitions (typically ₹10–₹100 each)
  • Most courts now accept online payments through state court portals or e-Courts services
  • Free legal aid or fee waivers may be available through the Legal Services Authorities if you cannot afford the fees
